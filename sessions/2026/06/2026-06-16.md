## Rolling Log

### [~03:30 EDT] Transition anxiety — first Hermes session

**Type:** motivational
**RQ:** RQ5
**Tags:** anxiety · executive-function · environmental-design · meta-cognition

Subject launched first live Hermes (Virgil) session after a full setup day. Reported anxiety about the transition, specifically about whether the new tooling would match the quality of established collaboration. The anxiety is functionally specific: not about the new tool being bad, but about losing a working relationship and workflow that has been yielding good outputs. This is the "loss of working environment" variant of ADHD environmental sensitivity — when a setup works, changing it carries disproportionate felt risk even when the change is self-initiated and reversible.

---

### [~05:11 EDT] Decision paralysis — tool selection, quality vs. flexibility trade-off

**Type:** behavioral
**RQ:** RQ5
**Tags:** decision-paralysis · executive-function · meta-cognition · anxiety · confidence-calibration

Subject explicitly named a recurring pattern: difficulty making tool and workflow decisions when multiple valid options exist, fear of making the wrong choice, and getting stuck rather than proceeding. Direct quote: "This is one of the things I struggle with — decision making, concerns over making the wrong decision, and figuring out exactly the best tool and process for my use case." The specific stuck point: choosing between Claude Code (high quality, subscription) and Hermes/OpenRouter (flexible, cost-variable, persistent memory). Subject recognized the either/or framing as false but could not self-resolve it without external reframing. The paralysis occurred after significant productive setup work — consistent with the earlier observation that temporal anxiety and negative affect emerge *after* high-output periods, not only during stagnation. Decision paralysis here is a cognitive-load endpoint, not an entry state.

---

### [~05:11 EDT] Extended Mind anxiety — fear of access loss

**Type:** identity
**RQ:** RQ5
**Tags:** anxiety · meta-cognition · executive-function · confidence-calibration

Subject articulated a contingency-planning concern: maintaining access to AI tooling even if the primary subscription (Claude Max) became unavailable. Framed as wanting flexibility rather than dependency. This is a direct extension of the earlier Extended Mind Thesis observation (2026-06-15): the fear is not of the tool itself but of the loss of the tool, which functions as cognitive infrastructure. The concern is structurally rational — building a redundant pathway (OpenRouter/Hermes) is a genuine risk mitigation — but the affective weight attached to it (significant anxiety, standing-still feeling) is disproportionate to the actual risk, which is low and reversible. The overconcern is the data point, not the concern itself.

### [~05:11 EDT] Output quality as identity stake — research integrity concern

**Type:** identity
**RQ:** RQ5 · RQ1
**Tags:** confidence-calibration · meta-cognition · anxiety

Subject's stated concern was not cost or convenience but quality: "I'm more concerned with accuracy, factual data, and rigorous research and deep dives." The concern about hallucination, factual inaccuracy, lack of depth, inconsistency, and drift was named precisely and in domain-appropriate terms. This is significant: the anxiety is organized around research integrity, not personal performance. The self-standard is external and rigorous — "publishable quality, factually defensible" — which is appropriate for the autoethnographic research methodology but raises the affective cost of any perceived quality risk. The quality concern is valid and was honored (tool routing was revised to preserve Sonnet-class models for all content generation). The intensity of the concern relative to the actual risk is itself RQ5 data on how ADHD affects decision-making under quality pressure.

### [~05:30 EDT] Cost analysis resolves decision paralysis — stick with Claude Code

**Type:** behavioral · decision
**RQ:** RQ5
**Tags:** decision-paralysis · cost-analysis · tool-selection · executive-function

Explicit cost analysis performed from observed API data ($0.40–0.82 per turn at Sonnet pricing via OpenRouter, with 3–5 API calls per user turn each carrying the full accumulated context payload). Projected $100–250/month at regular usage — equivalent to the Claude Max subscription at higher friction. Decision made: Claude Code as primary daily driver; Hermes reserved for background automation and emergency fallback only. Notable pattern: decision paralysis (named explicitly earlier in this session) dissolved immediately when presented with concrete cost numbers. The ambiguity that sustained the paralysis was uncertainty about value tradeoff under incomplete information; hard data resolved it in minutes without deliberation. Consistent with ADHD executive function literature where concrete, quantifiable information reduces comparative decision cost by eliminating the "what if I'm wrong" loop.
