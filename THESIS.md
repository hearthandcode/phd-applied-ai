## Central hypothesis

Augmented intelligence — AI operating as a bidirectional cognitive extension, calibrated
to a learner's individual personality and cognitive profile — enables learning outcomes
that traditional institutional models of education failed to produce. For neurodiverse
learners whose cognitive profiles create significant friction at the task-initiation and
conception-to-execution interface, this augmentation does not merely improve outcomes:
it enables doctoral-level engagement that was previously structurally inaccessible.

**Derived:** 2026-06-14, through Socratic introspective dialogue. Grounded in lived
experience as the primary research subject (RQ5). Connects to Clark & Chalmers'
Extended Mind Thesis (1998) as theoretical foundation.

**Key terms:** augmented intelligence · cognitive extension · task-initiation interface ·
conception-to-execution gap · neurodiverse learner profile · self-directed doctoral study

## Research questions

**RQ1 — Adaptive competency modeling**
How can adaptive AI systems model individual learner competency trajectories in real time,
and what instructional design principles govern their effectiveness?

**RQ2 — Pedagogy and generative AI**
What pedagogical frameworks are most effective when mediated by generative AI tutors, and
how do they compare to traditional human-mediated instruction?

**RQ3 — Virtue, vice, and learner motivation**
Can computational models of personality, affect, and values — specifically virtue/vice
frameworks — improve motivation and learning outcomes in AI-native educational environments?

**RQ4 — Governance and ethics at scale**
What governance, ethics, and fairness constraints are necessary for AI systems that assess
and adapt to individual learners at scale?

**RQ5 — Neurodiversity and AI-mediated learning (autoethnographic)**
How do a learner's personal characteristics — ADHD, bipolar disorder, cyclical energy,
variable recall, and task-initiation difficulty — shape their trajectory through AI-mediated
doctoral-level study, and what design principles follow from five years of that data?

*Methodology note for RQ5:* Longitudinal autoethnography. The researcher is the primary
subject. Claims are framed as generative hypotheses for future research, not generalizable
theory from N=1. Appropriate methods: structured self-report, thematic analysis of audit
logs, design principles grounded in lived experience.

## Thesis contribution

1. A theoretical framework for adaptive AI-native learning systems integrating competency
   modeling, affective tutoring, and virtue/vice-aware learner modeling
2. A working implementation (Hearth & Code platform) as the research laboratory
3. Evidence that augmented intelligence — AI calibrated to an individual's cognitive and
   personality profile — enables doctoral-level learning outcomes that institutional structures
   failed to produce for the same learner, specifically through resolving the
   task-initiation and conception-to-execution interface failures that institutional models
   do not accommodate
4. A 5+ year longitudinal dataset of a neurodiverse adult learner using AI as a primary
   educational tool — one of the first such datasets in the AIED literature
5. Design principles for adaptive systems that respond to cognitive/affective state fluctuation
   in neurodiverse learners, derived from the lived autoethnographic record

## Research methodology

*This section establishes foundational methodological principles for the full dissertation. A complete methodology chapter is developed in M62. This document captures core commitments as they were established at the outset — before substantive data collection began — so that methodological decisions can be traced to their original framing.*

### Methodological approach: longitudinal autoethnography

This research adopts **autoethnography** as its primary methodology (Ellis & Bochner, 2000; Chang, 2008; Adams, Holman Jones & Ellis, 2015). The researcher is the primary — and currently only — research subject. This is not a methodological convenience but a deliberate choice grounded in the research question itself: RQ5 asks how a specific learner's neurological and cognitive profile shapes their trajectory through AI-mediated doctoral study. The only way to access that data with the granularity and longitudinal depth that RQ5 requires is direct first-person documentation.

**Epistemological position:** This research claims *theoretical transferability*, not statistical generalizability. The goal is not to produce findings true for all neurodiverse learners — that would require a different design. The goal is to produce a rich, detailed account of one learner's trajectory that:

1. Reveals mechanisms and patterns not previously documented in the AIED literature
2. Generates hypotheses testable in future research
3. Offers design principles grounded in lived experience that practitioners can evaluate against their own contexts
4. Contributes to the thin empirical literature on neurodiverse adult learners in AI-mediated educational environments

This aligns with published autoethnographic standards for trustworthiness (Lincoln & Guba, 1985): credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability. Member-checking and peer debriefing are pursued through researcher outreach and the public research record.

### Conflict of interest disclosure

The researcher occupies four roles simultaneously: **researcher**, **primary research subject**, **curriculum designer**, and **commercial product builder**. Each role creates potential conflicts with the others. This document commits to naming them explicitly rather than minimizing them.

**Researcher ↔ product builder:** Research findings could be unconsciously interpreted in ways that support the commercial platform. Mitigations adopted:
- Research questions and the central hypothesis were established and committed to the public record before substantive data collection began
- All data, methods, and findings are published in full — including negative findings, off days, and design failures
- External researcher engagement (peer debriefing) is a formal part of the process, not optional
- The research-drives-product principle (below) is a standing commitment on public record

**Researcher ↔ curriculum designer:** The researcher designs the curriculum they study from. Mitigation: the design evolution is itself logged as data (CHANGELOG.md, session logs, generation guides). Adjustments can be traced and examined. Adaptive curriculum design in response to a learner's profile is part of the phenomenon under study, not a confound.

**Subject ↔ commercial interest:** The researcher's personal learning experience informs a commercial platform. The platform's design claims will distinguish explicitly between: (a) principles derived from the N=1 autoethnographic record (labeled as such), and (b) principles validated by independent research or external data (labeled as such). The platform will not represent autoethnographic findings as validated at scale without independent evidence.

### Research-drives-product principle

The research program has priority over the product vision. This is a deliberate inversion of the more common path (build a product, then research to validate it), and it is a foundational commitment.

In practice:
- If research findings contradict an existing platform design decision, the platform design changes — not the findings.
- If evidence suggests a design principle does not hold for the intended learner population, that finding is published and the platform is adjusted, even if the adjustment is commercially costly.
- Platform scope, feature set, and pedagogical model are treated as hypotheses to be tested, not conclusions to be defended.

The motivation is both scientific and personal. Scientifically: a platform whose design is locked before evidence is gathered is a product, not a research contribution. Personally: this platform is designed to serve learners who have been failed by structures that prioritized institutional convenience over learner needs. Building another such structure — even with good intentions — would betray the research's central claim.

This principle does not exclude commercial viability. Effective pedagogy and good products are generally aligned. Where evidence and commercial convenience conflict, the evidence wins. This commitment is part of the public record.

### Data collection principles

**Personal data (researcher as subject):** The researcher's psychological, cognitive, and behavioral data — collected through the session log system — is published with full consent and disclosure. This is the primary RQ5 corpus. No anonymization of the researcher's identity is applied; the researcher's disclosure is the contribution. Data categories: energy level, focus quality, mood, cognitive load, study hours, competency assessments, behavioral observations, design insights.

**Public data (web, literature, open datasets):** Data from publicly available sources used for literature review and synthesis requires no ethics review, consistent with academic research standards.

**Platform user data (future):** When Hearth & Code begins serving users, any data collection from those users — including anonymized usage logs — will require:
1. A formal privacy review and IRB-equivalent ethics process before collection begins
2. Explicit informed consent, with clear disclosure of research use
3. Compliance with applicable data protection law (GDPR, CCPA, and equivalents)
4. Separation between research data and commercial analytics in both storage and use

No platform user data will be collected for research purposes without these conditions met. This is a standing commitment, not a future consideration.

### Grounding in lived experience

This research is rooted in a specific kind of knowledge that academic research frequently excludes: the knowledge of what it feels like to want to learn and not be able to — because the structure of the learning environment is incompatible with how your mind works.

The researcher has experienced this directly: in conventional educational programs where the response to difficulty was "spend more time reading," in self-directed study without scaffolding, and in the gradual discovery that AI-mediated tools began to resolve barriers that years of effort had not. This is not anecdote. It is the phenomenon under investigation: the mechanism by which AI augmentation enables learning that institutional structures failed to produce.

Grounding in lived experience does not make research less rigorous. It makes it irreplaceable. No external researcher can generate this data. No survey can capture this trajectory. The legitimacy of this research rests on treating lived experience with the rigor it deserves — structured data collection, consistent vocabulary, verification, public record, and genuine peer engagement.

The goal is not to validate a product or to tell a satisfying story. It is to produce evidence that practitioners, researchers, and learners can engage with honestly — including the parts that don't work, the design failures, and the open questions that five years of data may not fully resolve.

## Key concepts and terms

**Augmented intelligence** — AI operating as a bidirectional cognitive extension calibrated
to a learner's individual personality and cognitive profile. Distinct from "artificial
intelligence" (which implies replacement or independence from human cognition) and from
general "AI assistance" (which implies one-directional tool use). Augmented intelligence
describes a composite — learner mind and model — that is more capable than either component
alone, specifically because the AI mediates the gap between what the learner can conceive
and what they can execute. Term coined for this research; connects to Clark & Chalmers'
Extended Mind Thesis (1998).

**Conception-to-execution interface** — the cognitive gap between generating an idea or
understanding a concept (conception) and producing a concrete artifact or implementation
(execution). For neurodiverse learners with task-initiation difficulty, this interface is
the primary site of failure in traditional education: intellectual capacity is present;
the pathway from thought to output is blocked. Augmented intelligence closes this interface.

**Task-initiation difficulty** — a cognitive trait associated with ADHD and related
neurodiverse profiles in which beginning a task — even a desired, well-understood task —
requires disproportionate effort relative to executing it once started. Distinct from
motivation failure or inability. AI scaffolding that externalizes the first steps resolves
this without requiring the learner to overcome it through will.

**Neurodiverse learner profile** — for purposes of this research: a learner profile
characterized by variable attention, cyclical cognitive capacity (not consistent across
days or phases), high conceptual fluency paired with implementation-layer friction, and
late diagnosis of ADHD and/or Bipolar disorder. Not a deficit model — a profile with
distinct strengths (systems thinking, conceptual synthesis, hyperfocus) and specific
interface incompatibilities with conventional academic structures.
