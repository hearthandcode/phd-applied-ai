## First-pass terrain

The thesis now has four linked conceptual tasks:

1. Show when an AI scaffold satisfies Extended Mind coupling conditions, and when it fails.
2. Formalize monotropic containment as a possible scaffold affordance.
3. Make autoethnographic credibility depend on thick description, reflexivity, and audit trail.
4. Ground cognition-as-identity in embodied, enactive, disability, and neurodiversity frameworks without overclaiming.

## Anchor sources already checked

### 1. Extended Mind and coupling conditions

**Clark and Chalmers, "The Extended Mind"**

Source checked: `https://consc.net/papers/extended.html`

Useful thesis role:

- Establishes active externalism.
- Provides the parity principle: if an external process functions like an internal cognitive process would, it may count as part of cognition.
- Provides the Otto notebook case and the coupling criteria around availability, accessibility, automatic endorsement, and prior endorsement.
- Gives the thesis its central test: is the AI scaffold part of the cognitive process, or merely useful assistance?

Immediate implication:

The session logs should not simply say the agent helped. They should record whether the system was reliably available, directly accessible, endorsed after reflection, previously endorsed as reliable, and integrated into the subject's actual thinking process.

### 2. Monotropism and attention allocation

**Murray, Lesser, and Lawson, "Attention, monotropism and the diagnostic criteria for autism"**

Source checked: `https://monotropism.org/murray-lesser-lawson/`

Useful thesis role:

- Frames autism through atypical strategies for allocating scarce attention.
- Contrasts monotropic and polytropic attentional strategies.
- Gives theoretical vocabulary for deep or tight focus, high arousal interests, difficulty with shifting attention, and the importance of starting from the person's own interests.
- Supports the idea that an effective scaffold may work by routing from the person's active interest rather than imposing external attention targets.

Immediate implication:

The novel contribution should be framed carefully: in this longitudinal record, the AI scaffold appears to contain monotropic attention and cycle it back toward a central axis. That is not yet a general claim about all autistic or neurodivergent cognition. It is an N of 1 pattern that can generate future design hypotheses.

### 3. Trustworthiness and thick description

**Lincoln and Guba evaluative criteria summary**

Source checked: `http://www.qualres.org/HomeLinc-3684.html`

Useful thesis role:

- Establishes credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability as qualitative trustworthiness criteria.
- Supports the external feedback's point that transferability comes from thick description, not statistical generalization.
- Gives a way to answer the generalizability critique: readers judge transferability from rich contextual detail.

Immediate implication:

Session logs need more phenomenological texture: before state, friction point, scaffold feature, after state, and coupling status. A thin summary does not give readers enough material to judge transferability.

### 4. Enactive, ecological, and disability framing

**Jurgens, "Body social models of disability: Examining enactive and ecological approaches"**

Source checked: `https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1128772/full`

Useful thesis role:

- Connects enactive and ecological approaches with disability and neurodiversity paradigms.
- Challenges purely individualist or medicalized accounts of disability.
- Supports an environment-person fit framing: disability and cognition emerge through organism, body, social setting, and environment.
- Gives a nearby theoretical grounding for cognition-as-identity, especially if the thesis frames identity as lived cognitive style situated in a world of affordances.

Immediate implication:

The thesis should avoid saying cognition is identity as a universal metaphysical claim. A safer formulation is: for this subject, cognitive style is identity-relevant because the way the subject thinks structures agency, authorship, attention, and participation in the world.

## How to frame cognition-as-identity

Safer claim:

> For this subject, cognition is not merely an internal ability used to complete tasks. Cognitive style is identity-relevant because it shapes how the subject experiences agency, authorship, belonging, failure, and self-expression.

Possible theoretical supports:

- Extended Mind: cognitive process can include coupled external resources.
- Enactive cognition: mind is enacted through organism-environment activity, not isolated in a brain-bound container.
- Embodied cognition: cognition is shaped by bodily and affective state.
- Neurodiversity and disability studies: atypical cognitive styles are not simply deficits, but ways of being in relation to environments that can support or disable them.
- Monotropism: attention allocation can be a core feature of lived cognition, not an incidental preference.

What not to claim yet:

- Do not claim all neurodivergent adults experience cognition as identity in the same way.
- Do not claim AI tools are generally extended minds for neurodivergent users.
- Do not claim monotropic containment is established across the population.
- Do not treat anecdotal observations from AI communities as evidence. Treat them as signals that justify future study.

## Emerging trend hypothesis

A careful version of the emerging-trend claim:

> Informal observation suggests that some neurodivergent AI users are adapting LLM systems as personal cognitive infrastructure: context preservation, task initiation support, executive function scaffolding, working-memory relief, and voice translation. This should be treated as an emerging practice worth studying, not as a demonstrated population-level trend.

This is useful in the thesis introduction or future-work section, but only if framed as field observation and motivation for research.

## Article analysis report structure

Once the article is provided, analyze it with this structure:

1. **Citation and source status**
   - Full citation.
   - URL or file path.
   - Access status.
   - Whether the full text was available.

2. **Research problem**
   - What problem is the article trying to solve?
   - What gap does it claim?
   - Which field is it speaking to?

3. **Hypothesis or central argument**
   - What is the main claim?
   - Is it empirical, theoretical, methodological, or design-oriented?

4. **Methodology**
   - Study design.
   - Participants or data source.
   - Data collection method.
   - Analysis method.
   - Trustworthiness or validity strategy.
   - Reflexivity treatment.

5. **Findings**
   - Main findings.
   - Supporting evidence.
   - Limits and caveats.

6. **Future directions**
   - What the article says should happen next.
   - What it leaves unanswered.
   - What your thesis can contribute back.

7. **Fit with this thesis**
   - Extended Mind relevance.
   - Monotropism relevance.
   - Neurodivergent cognition relevance.
   - Autoethnography relevance.
   - Hermes/ExocoreOS implementation relevance.

8. **Open questions generated**
   - 5 to 7 questions that should shape the next methodology or literature pass.

## Initial open questions

These are provisional questions to use before the specific article is analyzed.

1. **Methodology:** How can the session-log protocol capture enough thick description to support transferability without creating logging fatigue that changes the phenomenon under study?
2. **Reflexivity:** When the AI scaffold decides a moment is meaningful enough to capture, how do we separate the subject's lived report from the tool's interpretation of that report?
3. **Coupling failure:** What are the strongest examples where the subject had to think around the AI rather than through it, and what do those failures reveal about the boundary between assistance and integration?
4. **Monotropism:** Does the scaffold actually contain monotropic focus, or does it sometimes disguise attention drift by producing artifacts that feel productive?
5. **Identity:** Which theoretical tradition best grounds the claim that cognition is identity-relevant here: enactivism, disability studies, neurodiversity theory, Extended Mind, or a synthesis?
6. **Generalization:** What features of this N of 1 record are likely subject-specific, and what features are plausible design hypotheses for a broader neurodivergent cohort?
7. **Implementation:** What telemetry can Hermes collect ethically and usefully without turning human meaning-making into automated surveillance?

## Contribution back to the corpus

If the longitudinal record is refined using the proposed protocol, the thesis may contribute:

- Design hypotheses for AI systems that support executive function without flattening neurodivergent cognition into neurotypical productivity norms.
