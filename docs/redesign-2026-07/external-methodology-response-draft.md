# Redacted Response Draft to External Methodology Feedback

## Use note

This draft intentionally does not name the external correspondent. Before sending, review for tone, privacy, and whether to include or omit the anecdotal note about other neurodivergent AI users.

## Draft

Thank you for this. Your reframing of monotropism was the piece that clicked for me.

I had been thinking of the AI scaffold mostly as a way to compensate for executive function gaps: task initiation, sequencing, memory, context recovery, and turning an idea into something executable. Your point helped me see something more specific and, I think, more theoretically interesting. In my case, the scaffold may not be correcting monotropic focus or pushing me toward a more typical distributed-attention pattern. It seems to be containing the intensity of that focus and cycling it back toward a central axis, so that the energy does not diffuse into ten adjacent threads, but also does not get punished for being deep, narrow, and persistent.

That distinction feels important. Conventional academic structures often enforce a kind of polytropism as you had mentioned: you mentioned deadlines, syllabi, committee meetings, course calendars, administrative sequence, and many externally imposed attention objects. What this system appears to do, at least for me, is different. It keeps returning me to one live thread, one table, one artifact, one next action. That makes the work feel less like I am being redirected away from my cognitive style and more like my cognitive style is being given containment architecture.

I am taking your credibility point seriously. The current logs are useful, but they often record what the scaffold did and then summarize why it mattered. They do not always capture the lived texture close enough to the moment: what the cognitive state felt like before the intervention, what kind of executive function failure was happening, what the AI response actually did that changed the situation, and what the state felt like afterward. I am revising the logging protocol so that when a high-signal moment occurs, the agent should ask a few short questions before writing a broad interpretive note. The default questions are: what was the cognitive state before, what was stuck or failing, what did the AI do that mattered, and what changed afterward.

I am also building the Extended Mind coupling criteria directly into the capture process. The logs should code not only for successes, but also for availability failures, accessibility failures, reliability failures, moments where I did not endorse the output, moments where the result felt alien to my cognition, and moments where using the scaffold increased cognitive load instead of reducing it. That seems essential if I am going to make a serious claim about functional integration rather than useful assistance. The failures may end up being the strongest evidence for the boundary of the claim.

Your reflexivity question is also exactly right. The scaffold is not just the cognitive tool under study. It is also part of the data collection apparatus and, at times, part of the interpretive apparatus. I do not think I can remove that tension, but I can account for it more explicitly. The process I am moving toward separates subject report, tool-observed state, AI interpretation, and later human verification. The agent should not silently decide what matters. It should follow pre-specified trigger criteria, use a short interview protocol, preserve failure cases, and mark the difference between my self-report and its own interpretation. The final research record still requires my review and verification.

There is also a technical layer here that may become part of the contribution. The system is not just a chat window. It is an orchestrated workspace built around a persistent agent, project memory, session search, tools, structured documents, and a growing ExocoreOS layer that maps where things live and what safety gates apply. I am sketching a more formal implementation plan for this: a capture protocol, coupling-condition coding, continuity checks after long gaps, and eventually Hermes plugin work that can observe session events without replacing human authorship. The goal is not to make the AI narrate me more aggressively. The goal is to make the scaffold more accountable about when and why it records something.

On generalization, I agree that this cannot claim population-level findings from one person. I am trying to frame the N of 1 record as a generator of design hypotheses. Thick description is the bridge: if the logs are rich enough, other neurodivergent readers and researchers can judge whether any part of the pattern transfers to their context. A future phase would need a broader cohort. For this thesis, I think the honest claim is that the longitudinal record can identify candidate mechanisms and design principles, especially around conception-to-execution friction, monotropic containment, trust, and authorship preservation.

Your point about cognition and identity is also pushing me to be more precise. I have been using that phrase because it feels true to the lived experience: the way I think is not an accessory to the work, it is the condition under which the work becomes possible or impossible. But I agree it needs grounding. I am looking at enactive and embodied cognition, disability studies, neurodiversity theory, and Extended Mind as the likely theoretical neighborhood. I do not want to claim that all neurodivergent people experience cognition this way. I want to argue that for this subject, cognitive style is identity-relevant enough that changing the scaffold changes the conditions of agency and self-expression, not only productivity.

One tentative observation, which I would frame carefully, is that I have encountered other neurodivergent people in AI and Hermes-adjacent spaces who seem to be using AI in similarly personal and structurally inventive ways: not just to write faster, but to compensate for executive function gaps, preserve context, organize thought, reduce initiation friction, and make their own cognition more usable to themselves. I do not have data for that yet. It is an anecdotal signal, not evidence. But it makes me wonder whether this is an emerging practice that has not yet stabilized enough to be visible in the literature.

So my current next step is to revise the method before expanding the claims. The thesis needs a stronger theoretical section on monotropism, a clearer coupling-condition rubric, a more disciplined session-log protocol, and an explicit reflexivity section about the AI system being both scaffold and instrument. Your feedback helped me see that these are not side issues. They may be the heart of the contribution.

I would also like to read the adjacent article you mentioned and map it against the thesis frame. My plan is to summarize its method, hypothesis, findings, and future directions, then use it to sharpen the open questions this project can answer as a longitudinal N of 1 dataset.
